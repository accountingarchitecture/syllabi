Course Description

An introduction to the concepts and principles that underlie the identification, analysis, measurement, recording, and communication of financial information for use in decision making by external decision makers.

Required Materials

  1. Horngren’s Financial & Managerial Accounting by Miller-Nobles, Mattison, and Matsumura (Pearson, 6th edition) OR (if you will not be taking ACCT 221 in the future) Horngren’s Financial & Managerial Accounting: The Financial Chapters by Miller-Nobles, Mattison, and Matsumura (Pearson, 6th edition)
  2. Online access to MyAccountingLab

You should choose one of the four following options depending whether you want an ebook or print book and whether you intend to take CCT 221 in the future. Please note that when you go to purchase your book and access, the options may not be in the same order, so please make your selection based on ISBN number.

Course Learning Objectives

Introduction to Financial Accounting will provide you with a basic understanding of the fundamental concepts and principles of accounting information and give you opportunities to apply your knowledge to analytic problems and cases. The course will highlight the ethical implications of reporting accounting information and making business decisions. In addition to emphasizing types of information needs, we will explore the inherent weaknesses/strengths of accounting information. After completing this course, a student should expect to:

  • Know how accounting information is used, along with other sources of information, to make financial and investment decisions.
  • Know the basic and most generally accepted accounting methods used to evaluate, record, and present an entity’s financial position and results of operations.
  • Utilize Excel (or similar software) and/or general ledger package as a tool in processing accounting information or as a tool in business decision making.
  • Apply Excel skills related to data entry, formatting, and validation to accounting problems.
  • Recognize the ethical implications of reporting accounting information and decision-making.
  • Demonstrate effective written communication skills through letters, memorandums, and/or reports.
  • Exhibit the ability to work together in teams to achieve common goals by organizing, coordinating, performing tasks, and promoting effective communication.

Exams:

The course includes four equally weighted examinations. Exam dates and times appear in the Detailed Tentative Schedule portion of this syllabus. During exams, you may use a calculator (cell phone and programmable calculators are NOT allowed) and a pencil. At the end of the semester, you may replace your lowest test score of the four tests with the same grade earned on the Final Exam if the Final Exam grade is higher than the lowest test score. Only exams that were attempted can have the grade replaced. Exams will be available during class time on the day reported in the schedule.

All exams will be proctored using Proctorio. Proctorio allows students with a webcam, a microphone, and a broadband internet connection to complete their exams while on camera; the service does not use live proctors but instead uses an automated algorithm to report on any exam session irregularities. Since this course has more than 2 proctored exams, selecting the "course fee" model instead of the "individual test fee" model when paying for the first exam will result in a price equal to two individual exams. Subsequent exams for that course bear no additional cost.

Quizzes:

In addition to homework, you will also have one quiz per chapter. Quizzes will generally be done through MyAccountingLab. The lowest two quiz grades will be dropped.

Arctic Blast Case:

The Arctic Blast project will be completed outside of class with students working in groups of three for most of the project. You may choose your group members. The case will involve a business startup with initial operating decisions, processing business transactions for a two-month period and generating and interpreting financial statements for that two-month period.

## Team Participation: An evaluation will be completed after the project to summarize each student’s performance as a member of the team. Where the evaluations for an individual suggest a team member failed to bear his/her share of the workload, the points earned by that student for the group project will be reduced to reflect the student’s level of effort.

Absence Policy

No student having a sanctioned absence shall be penalized for such absence on account solely of being absent. Nothing in this policy is intended to excuse a student from the responsibility to make up missed work within a reasonable length of time. Students with planned absences should notify me prior to an absence from class. Students with unanticipated absences should notify me as early as possible. Documentation of any absence may be required at my discretion.

If you must miss an exam due to a planned sanctioned absence, you will need to pre-arrange a time for you to take the exam. Otherwise, exams must be taken when scheduled except for unplanned sanctioned absences (which should be scheduled for make-up as soon as possible). Missed quizzes cannot be made up for any reason except in the case of a prolonged, medical absence from the university or other similar situation as determined by me. Late assignments and homework are generally not accepted given the length of time given to complete.

Definition of Sanctioned Absences: Absences of students from scheduled classes will be considered sanctioned for the following listed reasons:

  • Participation as a representative of the University in a scheduled intercollegiate athletic event.
  • Participation as a representative of the University in a scheduled professional/academic conference, academic competition, or a field trip scheduled as part of a course.
  • Participation as an officer of a University co-curricular organization in a scheduled conference for which participation is mandatory for the student (e.g., a required annual meeting for all presidents of a national student organization).
  • Professional or graduate school interviews as well as professional job interviews that cannot be rescheduled.
  • Health related absences.
  • Absences covered by Truman’s non-discrimination policy (e.g. special religious observances, military commitments).
  • Other situations not specifically noted in this list, but approved by me or the Provost.

Academic Honesty

Academic integrity is a standard set for this course. Either giving help to or receiving help from anyone else (whether that person is a member of our class or not) is a violation of this policy. Students are expected to complete all of their coursework and assignments individually unless otherwise assigned. Students are expected to work alone on the exams and quizzes without the use of any outside resources. Any writing assignment and spreadsheet assignment must be the exclusive work of the individual student as well. Students are encouraged to consult each other on homework assignments. Groups should work independently on the first two phases of the Arctic Blast Case and students should work individually on the analysis phase. Students are expected to be honest in their interactions with the professor. Violations will result in a zero on the offending assignment/exam and may lead to other penalties, including failure of the course. A student not upholding these expectations is subject to being reported to the Dean of Student Affairs Office and the Vice President for Academic Affairs. If you have any questions, please ask.
